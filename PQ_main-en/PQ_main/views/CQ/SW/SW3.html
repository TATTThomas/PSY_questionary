Imagery Training(Imagery Training)


Imagery training refers to imagine the practice of specific skills or actions without being in the actual field or actual manual operation. For example, the player imagine the experience of playing against the opponent and the strategy of the game.

According to the US Olympic Training Center (Murphy, Jowdy, & Durtschi, 1990), more than 90% of sports psychologists and Olympic athletes have used different forms of imagery training, and most of them consider that imagery training is helpful. There are also many Olympic coaches add imagery training to the athletes' training program.

Imagery training can activate the same neural network as in actual training. It can make the neural network more closely connected. A faster and more effective neural network will prompt us to become more proficient in a certain action. As a result, by imagining that you are practicing, you can indeed continue to improve your skills.

The Function

Imagery training is the use of various senses to create or reshape a certain experience in the mind to achieve relaxation, practice, and motivation.

The Principle

Imagery training emphasizes to be close to the real situation. The closer the user's imagination is to the real situation, the better the training effect. It is recommended that the content of image training include the following：

I) Real situation:

For example: Was it indoor or outdoor during the real game? Are there spectators? How about the weather? How does you feel in the field?

II) All of your senses:

(e.g., see, feel, hear, taste, smell) to rehearse your sport in your mind

III) Behaviors:

What do you do before and during the game? Warm up, Running, Jumping, Throwing, etc.

IV) Mental reaction:

How do you feel during the game? Are you nervous? Excited?

V) Physiological response:

Rapid heartbeat, sweaty hands, accelerated breathing, gastrointestinal motility, etc.

VI) Kinesthetic imagery:

Imagine the sensation of the muscles you will feel. For example, when shooting a basketball, imagine knees squatting slightly. When jumping, you can feel the contraction of the thigh and calf muscles; when you feel the elbow retracted in your hands, you can feel the arms and arms when you throw the ball. The sensation of muscle contraction in the forearm and wrist.

Notice:

1. Imagery training can help athletes, but it still cannot completely replace physical training. Many studies have pointed out that imagery training and physical training can achieve the maximum effect in parallel.
2. For novice, due to lack of actual experience, the effect of imagery training is small. It is recommended to focus on physical training first.

Reference
